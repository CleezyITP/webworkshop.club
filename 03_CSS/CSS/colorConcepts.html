Color Concepts

Terminology

  • Hue: what color something is, like blue or red
  • Chrome: how pure a color is, the lack of white, black or gray added to it
  • Saturation: the strength or weakness of a color
  • Value: how light or dark a color is
  • Tone:created by adding gray to a pure hue
  • Shade: created by adding black to a pure hue
  • Tint:created by adding white to a hue

Base and Accent colors

In order to understand the use of color on the web in a practical way, it is important to identify the difference between background, base and accent colors:
  • Background Color: This color is typically the most dominant and sets the general look and feel of the site. The default background is white. Some common examples are white, black, grey and blue.
  • Base Color(s): This color is used to break up the background color and is often a neutral color (white, grey, brown, and black colors). The base color normally contrasts with the background, and is used in menu elements, titles, subtitles, blocks of text and sometimes images/illustrations. A common example is a website with a white background dark grey base.
  • Accent Color: This color is used least but gives the site its personality. Your accent color is distinct from background and the base colors - and is typically the boldest color. The accent is typically found in logos, buttons, links, and sometimes headings, images, or illustrations. An example is a website with white background, the title and texts are dark grey and there is a big button in color red that reads “buy here”.

Color Contrast

WCAG 2.0 level AA requires a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 between fore and background colors, for normal text sizes (10-17px), and 3:1 for large text (24px). WCAG 2.1 requires a contrast ratio of at least 3:1 for graphics and user interface components (like buttons, inputs, and links). You can check the contrast of your color choices by using WebAIM's Color Contrast Checker.
